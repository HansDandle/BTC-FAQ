## Senate CLARITY Act Stalls Over Industry Disagreements

**Coinbase's sudden withdrawal of support for the Senate Banking Committee's rewritten CLARITY Act forced cancellation of a planned markup and exposed fragile consensus among industry stakeholders.**[1] The company cited four major concerns: expanded SEC authority over crypto markets, new Bank Secrecy Act obligations for decentralized finance protocols, stablecoin provisions that could restrict competition, and banking rules that disadvantage crypto-native payment products.[1] This timing—hours before the scheduled committee vote—amplified the political impact and signaled to lawmakers that industry unity cannot be assumed.

The Senate's approach differs fundamentally from the House-passed version. Rather than voting on the existing bill, the Senate Banking Committee is working from a complete substitute amendment that rewrites major sections.[1] This legislative rewrite carries significant risks: without Coinbase's backing, the bill's path forward depends entirely on bipartisan cooperation across both the Senate Banking and Agriculture committees.[1] Some observers predict political and procedural challenges could block passage before the November 2026 midterm elections, particularly if Democrats gain House control.[2]

However, not all industry voices oppose the effort. Ripple leadership and Andreessen Horowitz partner Chris Dixon argued that compromise remains possible and that delaying action could weaken the U.S. position in the global digital asset economy.[1] Senator Cynthia Lummis emphasized the importance of finalizing clear federal standards rather than abandoning the legislative process entirely.[1]

## Stablecoin Framework Moves From Theory to Practice

**The GENIUS Act, signed into law in 2025, establishes the first comprehensive U.S. stablecoin regulatory framework, with implementation deadlines accelerating throughout 2026.**[2] Supervisory agencies must publish implementing rules for dollar-backed stablecoin issuers by July 18, 2026, with full regulations taking effect by January 18, 2027.[2] The U.S. Department of the Treasury and FDIC have already begun public consultations, signaling that rulemaking will shape how issuers allocate compliance resources this year.[2]

This framework will have significant global implications. FinCEN will clarify anti-money-laundering and counter-terrorism financing obligations for stablecoin issuers, covering Travel Rule compliance and transaction monitoring—expectations that will likely inform FATF guidance and influence how other countries develop their own requirements.[2] The regulatory process balances robust risk reduction with growth-focused objectives, positioning the U.S. as the global standard-setter for stablecoin governance.[2]

## Enforcement Pivot Accelerates Industry Adoption

**The SEC has dismissed or closed at least a dozen crypto-related cases since January 2025, including litigated cases against Binance, Coinbase, and Kraken where courts had issued favorable rulings.**[3] This enforcement pullback reflects a broader regulatory shift: both SEC and CFTC leadership are prioritizing policy statements and guidance over enforcement actions.[2] Regulatory agencies have moved from an enforcement-focused approach to industry engagement, clearing the path for U.S. banks to expand crypto operations.[2]

This environment is accelerating institutional adoption. The OCC continues granting national trust banking charters to crypto-asset firms, further blurring the line between digital assets and mainstream finance.[2] Banks are announcing ambitious projects in custody, stablecoin issuance, reserve management, payments, staking, and tokenization—trends that will prompt other jurisdictions to prepare their own banking sectors for a digitized future.[2]
